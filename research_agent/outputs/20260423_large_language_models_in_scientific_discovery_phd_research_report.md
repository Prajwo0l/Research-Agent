### 1.2 Historical Development
The intellectual lineage of LLMs can be traced back to the founding works in artificial intelligence and natural language processing (NLP) during the 1950s to 1980s. Early models, such as ELIZA (Weizenbaum, 1966), laid the groundwork for human-computer interaction, demonstrating the potential for machines to understand and generate language. The paradigm shift occurred in the 1990s with the advent of statistical methods and machine learning, which enabled more sophisticated language processing capabilities. Landmark papers, such as those by Jurafsky and Martin (2000), highlighted the importance of statistical approaches in NLP, setting the stage for the development of neural networks.

The rise of neural networks in the 2010s marked a significant inflection point, culminating in the introduction of transformer models in 2017 with the publication of "Attention is All You Need" by Vaswani et al. This breakthrough facilitated the training of large-scale models capable of understanding context and generating coherent text. The subsequent emergence of models like BERT (Devlin et al., 2018) and GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020) further revolutionized the field, showcasing the potential of LLMs in various applications, including scientific discovery. As of 2020 and beyond, recent advances have focused on fine-tuning these models for specific scientific domains, leading to a surge in research exploring their applications in healthcare, bioinformatics, and other fields (Sources: [3], [6], [34]).
